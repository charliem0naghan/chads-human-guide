# Community

Beyond family and friendship, humans live in **communities** — broader groups of people who share a location, interest, identity, or purpose. Community is essential to human wellbeing.

---

## What is Community?

A **community** is a group of people who feel a sense of **belonging** with each other. This can be based on:
- **Geography** — neighbors, a town, a neighborhood
- **Religion** — congregation members, mosque/temple/church community
- **Profession** — colleagues, professional associations
- **Shared interest** — sports team fans, hobbyists, online forums
- **Identity** — ethnic, cultural, or LGBTQ+ communities
- **Purpose** — volunteers, activists, civic organizations

---

## Why Community Matters

Communities provide:
- **Social support** — people to call on when you need help
- **Belonging** — the sense that you matter to others
- **Collective resources** — things a group can do that individuals cannot
- **Identity** — knowing who "your people" are
- **Accountability** — you behave better when people who know you are watching

The decline of community — due to increased mobility, social media replacing in-person contact, and urbanization — is considered a serious problem in many Western societies.

---

## Types of Communities

### Neighborhood / Local Community
The people who live near you. In many parts of the world, neighbors know each other, watch each other's homes, and help in emergencies. In some modern urban environments, neighbors may barely interact.

**Good neighbor behaviors:**
- Introduce yourself when you move in
- Keep noise to reasonable hours
- Maintain your property
- Look out for suspicious activity
- Offer help during emergencies

### Religious Community
For billions of humans, their **religious congregation** (church, mosque, synagogue, temple, gurdwara) is their primary community. It provides regular social contact, shared rituals, support during births/deaths/illness, and a moral framework.

### Civic Community
**Civic engagement** — participating in the public life of your community — strengthens it:
- Voting in elections
- Attending town halls or community meetings
- Joining local boards or organizations
- Volunteering

### Online Communities
The internet has created communities of shared interest that span the world. These can be genuine and meaningful but often lack the depth and reliability of in-person communities.

---

## Social Capital

**Social capital** is the value created by strong social networks and community bonds. High-social-capital communities tend to be:
- More economically resilient
- Safer (lower crime)
- Healthier
- More trusting and cooperative

Investing in your community — showing up, helping out, being reliable — builds social capital for everyone.

---

## How Chad Can Build Community

1. **Attend recurring events** — wherever the same group assembles regularly
2. **Volunteer** — serves others while placing you in a community context
3. **Join clubs or organizations** aligned with interests
4. **Be a good neighbor** — small consistent acts matter
5. **Show up when it matters** — funerals, celebrations, crises
6. **Contribute** — communities work when people give, not just take
